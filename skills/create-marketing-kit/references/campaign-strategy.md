# Campaign strategy reference

Use this reference to turn product evidence into a compact campaign system. Keep the working notes in the campaign kit so another person can audit or revise them.

## Pre-write truth audit

Collect only evidence that exists now:

| Field | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Product | What does it do today? |
| Audience | Who is explicitly served? |
| Tension | What present problem does the product address? |
| Mechanism | What concrete workflow creates the change? |
| Proof | Which screenshots, files, demos, or shipped behavior support it? |
| Boundary | Is it preview, beta, waitlist, local-only, paid, or otherwise limited? |
| CTA | What can a person truthfully do next? |

Tag each prospective claim:

- `verified`: directly supported by the product or an authoritative statement;
- `inference`: plausible, but word it as an interpretation rather than a fact;
- `unknown`: exclude it or ask for evidence.

Testimonials, adoption numbers, rankings, performance metrics, guarantees, and comparisons require direct evidence. A polished interface does not prove a feature works.

Privacy, security, encryption, and absolute data-residency statements require implementation-level confirmation. Check current configuration, telemetry, network behavior, third-party services, and platform backups where relevant. Until verified, record them as owner-supplied claims pending confirmation and qualify or omit absolute wording. If sources conflict, preserve the conflict and ask the product owner to resolve it.

## Campaign thesis

Write one sentence using this shape:

> For [audience] who [tension], [product] helps them [change] through [verified mechanism], so they can [human outcome].

Then compress it into a short campaign promise. The promise may be evocative, but its supporting copy must connect it back to verified product behavior.

## Message hierarchy

Build copy in this order:

1. **Promise** — memorable and human, usually 2–8 words.
2. **Value proposition** — one sentence explaining the practical change.
3. **Proof points** — two or three concrete capabilities or artifacts.
4. **Qualifier** — availability, preview status, limitations, or ownership model.
5. **CTA** — one honest next action.

Make each lower level substantiate the level above it. If the proof points cannot support the promise, revise the promise.

## Channel adaptation

Do not copy one block unchanged everywhere.

| Channel | Primary job | Recommended shape |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Website hero | Establish value quickly | promise, value, CTA, product proof |
| Editorial section | Create emotional identification | human scene, short story, one bridge to product |
| README banner | Set tone without hiding setup | concise promise, accessible image, immediate docs below |
| Launch post | Explain why now | tension, product change, proof, boundary, CTA |
| Social feed | Stop and orient | one idea, one visual metaphor, short caption |
| Story/reel cover | Read at a glance | short promise, strong subject, generous safe zones |

## Copy guardrails

Record these in the campaign README:

- approved product name and capitalization;
- words that overstate the current release;
- unavailable or experimental features;
- required privacy, local-first, preview, or pricing qualifiers;
- CTA destinations and their current behavior;
- claims that require a product owner to approve.

Prefer concrete verbs and objects over abstract hype. Remove claims such as “best,” “effortless,” “guaranteed,” or “revolutionary” unless evidence and context make them defensible.
