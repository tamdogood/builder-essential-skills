# Ethos

> Inspired by the overall philosophy of Pieter Levels' *MAKE: The Indie Maker Blueprint*. This is an original synthesis of that philosophy — for the full book in the author's own words, [buy it at readmake.com](https://readmake.com).

## The core stance: bootstrapping over venture funding

This whole system leans toward a specific, deliberate position: building self-funded, with your own skills and resources, and growing a small side project into something that can eventually replace your income — rather than raising money early and betting everything on a single high-growth outcome. The odds of a venture-backed startup returning meaningfully for its founders are stacked against most attempts; a self-funded business has a much lower bar for "success" (covering your own costs, then your own income, then more) and doesn't foreclose raising money later, from a position of proven revenue instead of just an idea.

## App, site, product, startup, business — treat these as roughly interchangeable

The underlying framework in this system applies whether what you're building is a website, a mobile app, a small tool, or a full company — the boundaries between these categories are blurry in practice, and most of the advice here doesn't depend on which label fits best.

## Persistence and realistic expectations

Most people who eventually build something that works have shipped many things that didn't first. If something clearly isn't gaining any traction after a real attempt, it usually won't gain traction later either — that's a signal to try something new, not to keep pushing the same thing indefinitely. The single highest-leverage behavior across this entire system is simply continuing to ship, especially right after something doesn't work.

Be aware of survivorship bias in any advice like this, including this system — what worked for someone else won't necessarily transfer directly to your situation, because you can't see every variable that contributed to their outcome. Treat this as a set of useful starting frameworks to test against your own reality, not a guarantee.

## The seven-stage model

Idea → Build → Launch → Grow → Monetize → Automate → Exit

1. **idea.md** — find a real problem, ideally your own, and size it honestly before committing.
2. **build.md** — build the smallest real version yourself, with what you already have.
3. **launch.md** — launch early, more than once, humbly, and to the audience that actually matters.
4. **grow.md** — grow from a genuinely good product and real transparency, not manufactured hype.
5. **monetize.md** — ask directly for money in exchange for value, honestly and without hidden costs to your users.
6. **automate.md** — automate repeatable work, and bring in people for what genuinely needs a human.
7. **exit.md** — if you exit at all, negotiate from a position of not needing to, and prepare for it as a real life transition.

## Ethics — a standing constraint on all of the above

Being ethical isn't a separate topic from building a good business — it's a constraint on how you pursue every stage above. Avoid spam, manipulative growth tactics, faked social proof, and using leverage unfairly once you have it. A genuinely good product doesn't need any of these shortcuts, and being visibly straightforward — including owning mistakes when you make them — is a real, durable advantage precisely because it's rarer than it should be.

## How this system uses that ethos

Advice anywhere in this system that would require faking engagement, misleading users, or a genuinely unfair tactic should be treated as out of bounds even if it would technically work — that's a deliberate value baked into this whole framework, not an incidental detail.
