# Grow

> Inspired by the "Grow" chapter of Pieter Levels' *MAKE: The Indie Maker Blueprint*. This is an original checklist/diagnostic built around that chapter's core ideas — for the full chapter and examples, [buy the book](https://readmake.com).

## Core principles

- **Be skeptical of "growth hacking" as a service.** Growth tactics can't fix a product nobody wants, and shortcuts that violate a platform's rules tend to get punished (bans, shadowbans, delisting) rather than rewarded.
- **Growth has to be built into the product, not bolted on afterward.** If people don't naturally want to come back and tell others, no tactic changes that durably. Treat tactics as an amplifier of something already working, never a substitute for it.
- **Flat organic growth is useful information, not just a problem.** If a product isn't spreading on its own, that's a signal to go improve the product or sharpen the differentiation — not a signal to immediately buy ads to paper over it.
- **Paid acquisition works best after there's real organic pull.** Spending on ads before you have a product people already want to use tends to buy temporary, disappearing usage rather than a durable business.
- **Never fake growth.** Bought followers, bots, or purchased engagement don't create real usage, and audiences are increasingly good at spotting (and turned off by) inflated numbers. Small and real tends to earn more goodwill than large and fake.
- **Transparency is a genuine growth lever.** Sharing real numbers, real mistakes, and real progress publicly gives people a reason to root for you and gives press/word-of-mouth something authentic to pass along — more than a polished announcement does.
- **Make sharing part of the product itself.** Wherever your product produces a specific, personalized result (a filtered list, a generated output, a completed action), making that result easily shareable — with a real preview, not a generic link — tends to convert into more social traffic than a single static landing page ever will.

## The two growth levers

1. **New users** — keep re-launching as the product evolves (see launch.md), tell the ongoing story publicly, make sharing frictionless, and consider whether integrating with other products (an API, a plugin, a marketplace listing) could put you in front of adjacent audiences.
2. **Returning users** — retention ultimately comes from the product being good enough that people choose to come back on their own; sparing, high-value communication (not spam); and, where relevant, a community that gives people a reason to stay engaged between uses.

## If you expose data or functionality to others

Letting other products integrate with yours (an API, embeddable widget, data feed) can be a real distribution channel — but it also makes it easier for someone to build a close copy using your own data. If you go this route, think deliberately about what you expose openly versus gate behind registration or rate limits, and monitor for obvious abuse.

## Measuring yourself against competitors

- Third-party traffic-estimation tools are useful for tracking *relative* trends over time, much less reliable as *absolute* numbers, especially for smaller sites — use them directionally, not as ground truth.
- Traffic isn't revenue. A competitor with more visits can easily have less profit than you if their monetization is weaker (see monetize.md).
- Useful competitive questions: What acquisition channel are they actually winning on? Are you competing for the same audience through the same channels, or should you be looking somewhere else entirely?

## Diagnostic questions to work through

1. Is growth actually organic right now, or propped up by something (ads, a one-time press spike) that stops the moment you stop pushing it?
2. What does retention look like a few weeks after someone's first visit — is the product itself pulling people back?
3. Are you sharing real progress and numbers publicly, or keeping the process private? What would change if you opened it up?
4. Is there a way to make your product's *output*, not just the product itself, personalized and shareable?
5. If you expose data or functionality to others, have you thought through the realistic risk of enabling a close copy versus the upside of distribution?

## A week of practice (optional homework)

Look at what actually happened after your last launch: did people who showed up keep using it, and where did they drop off? Pull together every piece of feedback you got — from any feedback channel, from launch-platform comments, from press if you got any — and make one concrete product change from it this week.
