# Launch

> Inspired by the "Launch" chapter of Pieter Levels' *MAKE: The Indie Maker Blueprint*. This is an original checklist/diagnostic built around that chapter's core ideas — for the full chapter, platform-by-platform tactics, and author's voice, [buy the book](https://readmake.com).

## Core principles

- **A launch can break a great product or save a mediocre one.** How you launch matters as much as what you built — treat it as its own project, not an afterthought once building is "done."
- **An MVP has to actually do something.** A landing page with an email signup is not a launchable product — visitors need to be able to use the core function the moment they arrive.
- **Launch early, and launch more than once.** Don't wait for one perfect big-bang moment. Every meaningful new feature is a fresh reason to tell people about the product again — some indie makers even spin a single feature out as its own standalone launch.
- **Launch humbly.** "Here's something I made that might be useful for you" lands better than "we're here to change the industry." Grandiosity from an unproven product invites backlash; humility from a real, working thing invites goodwill.
- **Tell the story, not just the feature list.** Why you built it and what problem it solves for you personally is more shareable than a changelog.
- **Building in public before launch pays off.** Sharing progress transparently — what's working, what's broken, what you're learning — builds an audience that's primed to show up on launch day, and gives press something more human to write about than a feature announcement.
- **Never fake engagement.** Bought followers, upvotes, or reviews are usually detectable, actively punished on some platforms, and undermine the trust you're trying to build. Real, small, and honest beats fake and large.
- **Make it actually shareable.** Basic social preview metadata (so a shared link shows a real title/description/image instead of a bare URL) meaningfully affects whether people click through.

## Pre-launch checklist

- [ ] Walk the entire user flow yourself, end to end, as if you were a stranger seeing it for the first time
- [ ] Get a handful of outside people to test it before the public does — you're too close to see what's confusing or broken
- [ ] Add an email capture that promises something specific, not a generic "join our newsletter"
- [ ] If it's a native app, use push notifications sparingly — overuse trains people to disable them
- [ ] Instrument basic analytics (traffic, referrers, core funnel) before launch day, not after
- [ ] Add a lightweight feedback channel so you can catch confusion or bugs live during the traffic spike
- [ ] Test that shared links render a real preview (title, description, image) on the platforms your audience actually uses
- [ ] Confirm your hosting can handle a real traffic spike, or have a lightweight static fallback ready

## Where to launch

Think in two categories:

- **General maker/startup communities** (e.g. Product Hunt, Hacker News, relevant subreddits, niche directories) — good for visibility, feedback, and a chance at press pickup, but the audience isn't necessarily your target customer.
- **Wherever your specific audience actually already gathers** — a niche forum, a specific subreddit, a professional community, a local group. This is usually higher-intent traffic even if the raw numbers are smaller.

A few practical notes that generalize across most startup-community launch platforms: submissions read as more authentic when framed casually ("I made a thing that...") rather than promotionally; timing matters on platforms with daily/rolling leaderboards; and server load can spike hard and fast, so a simple, fast-loading landing experience matters more than a feature-rich one on launch day itself.

## Getting press

- Generic "contact us" outreach mostly goes nowhere. Find the specific journalists or writers who already cover your niche or your closest competitors, and pitch them directly and briefly.
- A short, personal, no-hype email with a working link converts far better than a long, formal pitch — expect most outreach to go unanswered, and budget accordingly (send more targeted pitches than you think you'll need).
- Make it trivially easy to say yes: have screenshots and a one-line description ready to hand over, and reply quickly if someone follows up.
- "New" is what makes something newsworthy — a product with a clear point of view or an unusual angle gets covered more than one that plays it safe.

## Diagnostic questions to work through

1. Which platforms does your *specific* target audience actually use — not just the largest generic launch communities?
2. Have you personally walked through the entire onboarding flow as a stranger would, right before asking this question?
3. Do you have basic analytics and a feedback channel live before traffic arrives, not added afterward?
4. Can your hosting survive a real concurrent-traffic spike, or do you need a lighter fallback for launch day?
5. Do you have a short list of specific journalists or communities that cover your niche, rather than a generic press list?

## A week of practice (optional homework)

Write down every place you plan to launch, with a title and description tailored to that specific audience. Walk the full flow multiple times to catch bugs. Then pick a date and launch.
