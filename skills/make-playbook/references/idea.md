# Idea

> Inspired by the "Idea" chapter of Pieter Levels' *MAKE: The Indie Maker Blueprint*. This is an original checklist/diagnostic built around that chapter's core ideas — for the full chapter, examples, and author's voice, [buy the book](https://readmake.com).

## Core principles

- **Solve a problem you personally have.** You already understand it in more depth than an outside observer ever could, which means you'll build the right thing faster and notice when it's wrong sooner.
- **First-hand experience beats research — including after launch.** Once you stop being your own user, you risk drifting out of touch with what your audience actually needs. Deliberately stay close to the problem, or keep re-immersing yourself in it.
- **Original experiences produce original ideas.** If you keep landing on the same ideas everyone else has (another to-do app, another friend-finder), that's usually a sign your day-to-day inputs are common, not that you lack creativity. Change what you do and see, and your problem space changes with it.
- **Start from a real problem, never from a piece of technology.** New tech tempts people to build a solution first and go looking for a problem to attach it to afterward. Flip it: find the friction first, then decide if any technology actually removes it.
- **Ideas are cheap; execution is what creates value.** Don't hoard ideas out of fear someone will "steal" them — ten people with the identical idea will build ten different things, and the market only ever rewards the one that got built and iterated well. Share ideas early to get real reactions, not to protect them.
- **The market is the only real validation.** Opinions on an idea — including your own — are unreliable. Whether real people use it and pay for it is the only signal that actually counts.
- **Avoid ideation-by-committee.** Groups tend to talk each other into confidence about an idea in a way that has nothing to do with whether real users want it. Do early idea generation mostly solo, and bring outside people in to react to something built, not to vote on something imagined.

## Sizing an idea: the niche ladder

A useful way to size an idea without either underselling it or scoping it into "change the world" territory:

1. **Micro niche** — go narrower than feels comfortable. A product for "everyone who does X" is usually both too competitive and too vague to market. A product for a specific, definable slice of that group (a particular sub-audience, region, or use case) is small enough that bigger players ignore it, and specific enough that you can describe exactly who it's for and where to find them.
2. **Multi-niche** — once the first micro niche is validated, expand sideways into adjacent micro niches that the same core product already mostly serves.
3. **Adjacent markets** — once you own several related niches, look at what else those same customers need, and whether your existing relationship with them makes you the natural provider.
4. **Platform** — with enough adjacent markets served, look for the shared underlying capability you could offer as infrastructure to all of them at once.

Most large companies followed something like this path rather than starting with the ambitious end-state as their pitch — the size of the eventual company says nothing about how small and unglamorous the starting point looked.

### Quick math for sizing a niche

Before committing to an idea, sanity-check whether the niche is actually big enough to matter. Two ways to hit a meaningful annual revenue target, depending on pricing model:

**One-time purchase** — revenue ≈ price × customers. A handful of reference points: a few hundred customers at a several-thousand-dollar price point, or tens of thousands of customers at a ten-dollar price point, land in the same rough revenue range. Pick the price/volume combination that matches how your audience actually buys.

**Recurring subscription** — a modest, realistic-sounding subscription price times a few thousand subscribers compounds to a serious annual number faster than most people expect, because it's recurring rather than one-off. Model your own numbers: (price/month × 12 × subscriber count), and check that your niche plausibly contains enough people to hit the subscriber count you need.

The exercise is less about hitting an exact number and more about forcing yourself to ask "is my target niche actually big enough, at a price my audience will actually pay, to be worth building?" — before you spend months building it.

## Idea does not need to be huge on day one

Don't wait for a billion-dollar-sounding idea before starting. Almost every large company's real starting point was small, narrow, and easy to dismiss — the ambitious long-term version showed up only after years of iterating on user feedback from that small starting point. Optimize for "smallest real thing I can ship this month," not "biggest vision I can pitch."

## A simple idea-tracking system

Keep a running, low-friction backlog of ideas, split into stages so you can see where each one actually is instead of relying on memory:

- **Concepts** — raw, unfiltered brainstorm. No idea too weird; judgment kills the backlog before it has a chance to mature.
- **Promising** — concepts that survived an initial gut-check against the sizing/originality questions above.
- **Building** — currently in execution.
- **Success / Failure** — after the fact, with a one-line note on *why* — this becomes your own personal pattern library over time.

Ideas often need to sit for weeks or months before they're ready to execute — that's normal, not a sign you're procrastinating. Revisit the backlog periodically rather than only acting on whatever idea is freshest.

## Diagnostic questions to work through

1. What's a problem YOU personally hit repeatedly — not a problem you've observed in an industry you're outside of?
2. Can you name the specific micro-niche (not the broad market) this would serve first? Who are the people in it, and where do they hang out online or offline?
3. What's the smallest, least "grand" version of this that could ship in days, not months?
4. Have you told anyone about this idea yet? If not, is that fear of theft, or fear of judgment — and does either one actually hold up?
5. Does your idea backlog have 15-20+ candidates to compare this against, or is this the only thing on your mind right now?

## A week of practice (optional homework)

Spend the next 7 days writing down problems you hit in daily life — small or big. Aim for 3 a day, so you have at least 21 candidate ideas by the end of the week, then run the top few through the diagnostic questions above.
