# Automate (with AI)

> Inspired by the "Automate" chapter of Pieter Levels' *MAKE: The Indie Maker Blueprint*. This is an original checklist/diagnostic built around that chapter's core ideas — for the full chapter and examples, [buy the book](https://readmake.com).

## Core principles

- **Prefer scripts and scheduled workflows over hiring for repeatable work.** Anything you do the same way more than a couple of times a week is a candidate for automation — whether that's custom code or a no-code workflow tool doesn't really matter.
- **Modern AI tools (LLMs, vision models, agents) have widened what counts as automatable.** Tasks that used to require human judgment — parsing messy input, summarizing, drafting a reply, classifying something — are now realistically scriptable in a way they weren't a few years ago.
- **Only automate when the time saved clearly exceeds the time spent building the automation.** This is the actual discipline, not "automate everything." A quick, occasional task usually isn't worth a complex automation; a frequent, time-consuming task usually is.
- **"Passive income" is mostly a myth — it's compressed effort.** Heavy upfront work followed by a period of relative ease is real, but the total effort doesn't vanish, it shifts earlier. Something will usually still need your attention periodically, right up until you actually sell the business.
- **Run a "bus test" on your own operation.** Would things keep running if you disappeared for a month? If not, you personally are the single point of failure, and that's worth deliberately fixing over time.

## What's usually worth automating first

- Anything you do on a fixed schedule already (data refreshes, reports, reminders)
- Anything that alerts you to a problem faster than you'd otherwise notice it (uptime/health checks, error alerts)
- Repetitive content or data pipeline work (pulling from external sources, reformatting, publishing)
- Routine financial admin (invoice collection, expense logging)
- Simple, well-defined customer support flows (account changes, cancellations, FAQs) — freeing up human attention for the edge cases that actually need it

## Where humans still fit

Some things aren't worth automating yet, or genuinely need judgment: unusual support situations outside your self-serve flows, new strategic decisions, and anything requiring real creative or ethical judgment calls. A workable pattern:

- Build self-serve flows for the common cases first, so a human is only needed for what falls outside them.
- For technical or support coverage beyond what's automated, contractors paid for actual time spent (rather than a full-time salary) tend to be a lower-commitment way to cover the gap, especially early on.
- Favor people who can work autonomously with clear alerts/triggers over people who need ongoing management — the goal is for the alerting to do the coordinating, not you.

## A simple build-out sequence toward the "bus test"

1. Automate the highest-frequency repeatable work first.
2. Bring in contract help (ideally more than one person, for redundancy) specifically to keep the automated systems running.
3. Set up alerting so that help gets notified automatically when something breaks, rather than you being the one who has to notice.
4. If the business is real enough to need it, have one trusted person with operational/financial access who can keep things moving day-to-day without you.
5. Prepay recurring infrastructure costs (hosting, domains) far enough ahead that the business survives even a period of total founder absence.

## Diagnostic questions to work through

1. What are the handful of tasks you personally repeat most often for this product right now, and roughly how much time does each cost you per month?
2. For each of those, would automating it actually save more time than building the automation would cost, at your current scale?
3. If you disappeared for a month, what would break first? That's your real bottleneck.
4. Do you have any monitoring at all on whether your existing automations are still silently working?
5. Is there a task that isn't automatable but is simple enough to hand to a contractor right now, freeing you up for what only you can do?

## A week of practice (optional homework)

For one week, track everything you do repeatedly and list it out. Rank the list by how easy each item would be to automate, and start with the easiest one. For tasks that can't be automated but are simple enough for someone else to do, look into finding a contractor to hand them to.
