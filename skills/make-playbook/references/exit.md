# Exit

> Inspired by the "Exit" chapter of Pieter Levels' *MAKE: The Indie Maker Blueprint*. This is an original checklist/diagnostic built around that chapter's core ideas — for the full chapter, negotiation detail, and author's voice, [buy the book](https://readmake.com).

## Core principles

- **Most inbound acquisition interest isn't real.** A large share of inquiries are either lowball offers or attempts to extract competitive information under the guise of "due diligence," with no real intent to close. Treat early interest skeptically until it's clearly substantiated.
- **Your negotiating power is strongest when you don't need the deal.** Not needing or wanting to sell lets you price confidently and walk away from anything unsatisfying. Needing to sell — burnout, declining numbers, fundraising fatigue — is the worst time to be negotiating, because it shows.
- **Don't rush an exit out of exhaustion.** The moments that make selling feel most urgent (a rough patch, tough competition) are often exactly the moments when you'll get the worst terms, because there will be fewer genuinely interested buyers and less leverage to push back.
- **Be cautious of earnouts.** Payment structures tied to post-sale performance targets mean continuing to work for a company you no longer control, with limited ability to ensure those targets are actually hit fairly. Cash upfront is simpler and safer wherever you have the leverage to ask for it.
- **Bootstrapped companies genuinely do get acquired for large sums** — this isn't only something that happens to venture-backed startups.
- **An exit doesn't automatically solve your problems.** Sudden liquidity and the loss of the daily structure/identity the business gave you are real adjustments, not hypothetical ones — worth planning for emotionally, not just financially.

## Filtering buyers

Not all interest is worth the same amount of your time. Roughly, from least to most worth engaging with:

1. **Non-serious lowball offers** — ignore and move on.
2. **Information-gathering under the guise of interest** — someone wants details about your performance, strengths, or customers without real intent to buy. If you're going to share anything sensitive, get a real commitment in writing first (a real deposit or breakup-fee arrangement filters this out effectively).
3. **Endless scheduling without progress** — repeated rescheduling with no concrete terms is usually a sign of a non-decision-maker filling time. Push for async, written communication to force concreteness.
4. **Serious but heavily formal** — real interest, but slow and legally heavy, typically from larger acquirers. Workable, but budget real time and legal cost.
5. **Serious and straightforward** — clear, friendly, motivated communication that moves quickly. This is the buyer worth prioritizing your energy on.

## Valuing your company

Combine a few approaches rather than relying on one:

- **A multiple of revenue**, scaled up for stronger growth — but weigh that against what you'd likely earn just continuing to run the business for several more years, since selling trades a large, uncertain future for a smaller, certain amount today.
- **What you're actually earning net of your own unpaid labor** — a buyer will need to pay someone (or several people) to do what you currently do yourself for free, and that cost reduces what the business is worth to them. Be aware this method structurally favors the buyer's position.
- **Recent comparable sales** of similar-size, similar-growth businesses in your space, as a rough real-world anchor.

## Negotiating terms

- An assertive but credible opening number tends to anchor the rest of the negotiation better than opening low or waiting for the buyer to name a price first.
- Cash is simpler and more valuable than stock in a private company, which is typically illiquid.
- Get advice from an accountant experienced specifically with acquisitions before finalizing deal structure — how a sale is structured can meaningfully change your after-tax outcome.
- Keep any non-compete narrow and specific rather than broad.
- Clarify upfront who's responsible for any issue that surfaces with the product after the sale — it's a real negotiation point, not a formality.

## Preparing before you're in talks

- Get tax/structure advice on your company well before a sale conversation starts — restructuring after a buyer is already at the table is much harder.
- Audit your dependencies for licenses that could create obligations for a buyer (some open-source licenses require you to release your own code if you use them) — this is the kind of thing that derails a deal late if it's found during technical due diligence instead of ahead of time.
- Keep your bookkeeping clean on an ongoing basis. Messy books are one of the most common things that stall or kill a deal, because they make a buyer wonder what else is disorganized.

## After a sale

- Give yourself a real, deliberate way to mark the transition — it's a genuine life milestone, not just a transaction, and treating it as one helps you process it.
- Take the emotional adjustment seriously. Sudden financial change plus the loss of the routine/identity the business gave you is a well-documented risk for founders, not a hypothetical one. Invest in identity and relationships outside the business, and don't hesitate to get real support around the transition if you need it.
- Think carefully before jumping straight into lifestyle inflation or straight into starting another company out of habit. Both are common; neither is automatically the right move for you specifically. Ask honestly whether you *want* to do the next thing or feel compelled to because you don't know another way to spend your time.

## Diagnostic questions to work through

1. Do you actually want or need to sell right now, or is a rough patch pushing you toward a decision made under bad timing?
2. Has this buyer asked for sensitive details before agreeing to any real commitment (deposit, breakup fee, signed terms)? That's worth treating as a caution flag.
3. What's your walk-away price and your non-negotiables, decided in advance rather than mid-conversation?
4. Have you checked your dependencies for problematic licenses, and is your bookkeeping clean enough to survive a real audit today?
5. If this sale closed tomorrow, what's your actual plan for the day after — have you thought about that as seriously as the financial terms?
